Is the Real Estate Market Slowing Down?

Real Estate Economics 101

By Fernando Razo

This, at times, can feel like it is quite literally the million-dollar question, and one that I get very often. Many people seem to be fearing the worst and the news can make it seem apocalyptic (cue the 2008 housing market crash fears). Let me start off by saying what’s happening right now is nothing like what happened during the last recession. Rather, our current market conditions can be explained by fundamental supply & demand market forces. So without further ado, Let’s get into it:

Is the real estate market slowing down? The short answer is: Yes

But it's not exactly what you think. Let me explain.

Demand

Obviously, you know interest rates have gone up. This causes a home buyer’s monthly payment to go higher. which causes a home buyer's monthly payment to go higher. All of the sudden that $1,200 payment on a $250,000 house turns into $1,600. As a result, the amount of money they can borrow to buy a house reduces (i.e. their buying power). They can no longer afford to buy a $250,000 house due to the $400 per month increase. This is making home buyers' second guess their decision to even buy a home. As a result, the demand for houses is slowing down or shrinking.

Supply

Home sellers have not slowed down; people still want to sell their homes for various reasons. In fact, some neighborhoods may seem like the number of sellers actually increase! This is causing the number of homes on the market, or the inventory, to rise. In other words, there is a significantly higher supply of available homes.
Now when these two forces collide, this causes home prices to cool off. What does this mean for your home value? It means that although you may see the value of your home to reduce in the short term, it doesn’t appear to foreshadow a crash. Contrary to 2008, where sub-prime mortgages were issued to high risk borrowers and eventual default on these loans that caused the crash, this dip in the market is primarily due to a temporarily high interest rates. Like always, the market will self-correct, and all will be right with the world.
